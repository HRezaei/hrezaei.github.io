The Magic of Numpad

Once upon a time, I noticed that if we type two four-digit numbers according to a circle on the numpad (as the arrows show in the picture), and calculate their GCD, we will get a coefficient of 11, whatever those numbers be, and whatever direction (either clockwise or anticlockwise) we go through! it is also true for two eight-digit numbers, or even for a four-digit and an eight-digit one! Some examples are provided in the picture below and a GCD calculator is there to ease your further trials.

Try yourself:

All the above examples are anticlockwiseüîÑ. You can now try yourself, with some 4-digit numbers in clockwiseüîÅ and you see the Greates Common Divisor is still divisable by 11. For example, $GCD(4785, 6589) = 11$. Then try 8-digit numbers clockwise, or 8-digit numbers with first four digits clockwise, and the second four digits anticlockwise, or vice versa. For instance, $GCD(78543652, 56984521) = 11$. You can also try some random order digits to see this pattern does not happen always for any two number entered in a casual way. It happens only if you enter numbers in clock or anticlock wise order and only if number of digits be a coefficient of 4 (i.e. 4x-digit numbers).

It seems funny, doesn't it?! I didn't find this pattern mentioned anywhere and unfortunately I couldn't develop a proof for it. I also shared it with some mathematics students, but they didn't pay attention. So I sent an email to Professor Adleman (the A in the famous RSA algorithm!) and asked him about the reason behind this. I also asked the question "Isn't it worthy for further investigation?"

The answer is quoted below as it was:

Hi
I can give you a hint.
Write numbers base 10 and then consider the powers of 10 modulo 11.
Good luck

Professor Leonard Adleman

And for the second question, he replied:
... yes such patterns are worth investigation. That investigation began with gauss and has gone very far and produced great mathematics through the centuries.

So if you too believe this is interesting and worthy, continue with the remaining of this post. It isn't a scientific proof, rather, a funny playing with numbers. We try to do what Professor Adleman has hinted and get a sense of what's behind this.

But before following the Adleman's instruction, you can try some random order digits to see this pattern does not happen always for any two number entered in a casual way. It happens only if you enter numbers in clock or anticlock wise order and only if number of digits be a coefficient of 4.

Now consider 47855896 as an example. Writing it base 10 we have
$47855896 = (4 \times 10^7) + (7 \times 10^6) + (8 \times 10^5) + (5 \times 10^4) + (5 \times 10^3) + (8 \times 10^2) + (9 \times 10^1) + (6 \times 10^0)$
and powers of 10 modulo 11 are as follows:
$({\color{green}4} \times 10^7) \bmod 11 = 7 = 11 - {\color{green}4}$
$({\color{orange}7} \times 10^6) \bmod 11 = {\color{orange}7}$
$({\color{green}8} \times 10^5) \bmod 11 = 3 = 11 - {\color{green}8}$
$({\color{orange}5} \times 10^4) \bmod 11 = {\color{orange}5}$
$({\color{green}5} \times 10^3) \bmod 11 = 6 = 11 - {\color{green}5}$
$({\color{orange}8} \times 10^2) \bmod 11 = {\color{orange}8}$
$({\color{green}9} \times 10^1) \bmod 11 = 2 = 11 - {\color{green}9}$
$({\color{orange}6} \times 10^0) \bmod 11 = {\color{orange}6}$
Did you notice the pattern? For the odd powers of 10, the modulo 11 is 11 minus base digit, and for the even powers it is just the base digit (What for numbers other than 11?!). So you are now able to calculate large numbers module 11 very quickly in your mind! For instance
$500,000,000,000,000,000 \bmod 11 = ?$
The above number is $5 \times 10^{17}$ and since 17 is odd, its modulo 11 is $11 - 5 = 6$. What for $47855896 \bmod 11$? Adding up the remainings calulated above, we will have $7+7+3+5+6+8+2+6=44$, and $44 \bmod 11 = 0$ so $47855896 \bmod 11 = 0$. OK?! the same is true for other numbers in the picture or any others entered in the same way.

OK, by far we have learnt that any four-digit number typed on numpad in üîÑ or üîÅ order, is divisible by 11. And we also learned a new rule which helps us calculate module 11 rapidly and that's fine, but the answer to our own question isn't clear yet, at least for me. What is the principle behind this arrangment of digits which makes every such a number divisible by 11 and hence GCD of each pair of those numbers a coefficient of 11? Maybe there is a simple and well-known justification for this, but the perspective I'm looking to this problem from, has precluded me from perceiving it! Whatever it might be, it sounds peculiar and strange at the moment. I would be happy if anyone can explain further, why this happens and what is the relationship between this layout and that result?!

What with other layouts?

So is there really something with the numpad and its layout of keys? Interestingly no! Although I encountered it while toying with numpad, and the title of this post might seem deceptive, you can alter the layout of numpad, and you will see the same pattern happens in many of them. For example, swapping the rows or columns of numpad interchangably, you'll end up with a new layout, and if you type two 4x-digit numbers in üîÑ or üîÅ order, their GCD will be a multiply of 11 (see the picture below).

These three layouts are different from that of numpad but they are similar in regard to GCD and 4x-digit numbers.
